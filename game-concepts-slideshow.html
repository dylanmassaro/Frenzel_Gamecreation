Dataset-Driven

Professor provides a naval dataset. Players study it to understand which crew compositions, tech levels, and artillery loadouts produce the best outcomes. Data-driven decisions on compass upgrades, crew upgrades, and ship builds.

3

3 Predictive Models

Combat: crew stats → win probability. Navigation: tech + crew → route success. Loot Discovery: explorer + maps → treasure yield. All regression-based on the provided dataset.

S

Same Starting Point

All 30+ players start in the same sea. No region selection — everyone begins equal. Battling is disabled in Round 1, giving players time to build before PvP opens up. The map expands as players invest in navigators and maps.

C

PvE + PvP Choice

Each turn, players choose: explore an island (PvE) or attack another player (PvP). If 2+ players go to the same island, they all fight — creating emergent conflict even among explorers. Ships never fully sink — they're damaged and must be repaired.

Gameplay Loop & Mechanics

X rounds of play. Every round: build → navigate → explore or battle → collect loot → repeat. All 30 players act simultaneously. Battling disabled Round 1.

STEP 1
3:00

Build / Edit Ship

Hire crew, upgrade tech & weapons, buy food. Doctor heals, Ship Builder repairs hull. Every night between rounds.

STEP 2
1:00

Navigate

Choose: island (PvE) or player ship (PvP). Nav Model determines success. Failed routes waste the turn.

STEP 3
2:00

Explore / Battle

PvE: Loot Model → treasure. PvP: Combat Model → winner takes loot. 2+ at same island = fight. AI curveballs fire.

STEP 4
2:00

Collect / Review

Loot tallied. Leaderboard updates. Combat logs. Damage reports. Plan next round's investments.

Key Mechanics

Same start: All players begin in the same sea, same position. No regional advantage.

Round 1 = build only: No attacking allowed. Forces everyone to invest before the PvP opens up.

Island collision: If 2+ players go to the same island, they all fight. Loot is split by combat results.

Ships never sink: Damaged ships lose stat effectiveness. Ship Builder repairs hull. No permanent elimination.

Food → morale: Low food = low morale = higher mutiny risk. A hidden variable that punishes players who ignore crew welfare.

AI Curveballs, Scoring & Learning

S

Storm

Extra nav check. Low compass/sailing master = lost turn. Weather-resistant builds buffered.

AI
K

Kraken Attack

Random ship hit. Combat Model determines damage. Weak ships lose crew. Rewards combat readiness.

AI
B

Bounty on Leader

AI fleet hunts #1 player. Leader must defend or lose 20% loot. Prevents runaway winners. Comeback mechanic.

AI
M

Mutiny

Triggered by low morale (low food, no doctor, overworked crew). Disables a random crew member for a round. Food investment prevents this.

AI
T

Treasure Fleet

High-value AI merchant appears. All players race to intercept. Best Nav + Combat combo wins the jackpot.

AI
F

Fog of War

Visibility drops. Ships without Explorers can't see islands or incoming attacks. Punishes unbalanced builds.

AI

Scoring

Primary: Total loot held at end of final round.

Earning: Island exploration (PvE), raiding players (PvP), budgeting efficiency (loot spent on upgrades = loot not on leaderboard).

Anti-AFK: Must choose action every round. No "skip" option. Idle ships get random low-success routes. Crew wages deduct each round regardless.

Learning Outcomes

Multi-model reasoning: Optimizing for 1 model underperforms balanced builds across all 3.

Feature importance: Dataset reveals which crew roles/tech have highest coefficients. Feature interactions matter (Gunner without Powder Monkey = low DPS).

Risk vs. reward: 60% chance of stealing 500 loot vs. 90% chance of earning 250 from exploration — expected value thinking.

Restaurant Wars

Run a competing bakery/restaurant. Allocate budget across design, menu, employees, technology, and ads. Regression model determines revenue. 30 players compete for the same customer base.

Dataset-Driven

Professor provides a restaurant dataset. Features: menu diversity, staff count, ambiance, equipment tier, marketing spend, pricing → resulting revenue and satisfaction. Players study this to understand which features drive customer traffic.

M

Predictive Model

Regression model runs under the hood. Player decisions feed in as feature inputs. The model computes customer count, revenue, and satisfaction. Coefficients come from the dataset — studying data = competitive edge.

C

Zone Competition

Players pick 1 of 4 zones (downtown, suburb, campus, mall). Each zone has different rent, foot traffic, and customer demographics. Multiple players in the same zone split customers based on attractiveness score.

A

Ad Auction

Each night, sealed-bid auction for ad placements: Radio (most effective), Billboard, Bench Posters. 30 players bid blind — highest bids win. Overbidding hurts budget for staff and tech.

Restaurant Wars

Features — Employees, Tech & Design

All features are adjustable each night during closing hours — including store design. Budget constraint forces tradeoffs across every category.

Employees — Expanded Roles

Chef / Baker

Produces baked goods. More chefs = faster production = more items served per hour. Tiers: line cook → sous chef → head chef (output multiplier).

Barista

Produces drinks. Skilled baristas make specialty drinks faster. Tiers affect quality (satisfaction) and speed (throughput per hour).

Cashier

1 per register. Checkout speed affects queue length. Long queues = customers leave. Upgradeable register system multiplies cashier speed.

Busser / Janitor

Cleans tables for seating turnover. Dirty tables = lost seats. Also affects cleanliness score — low cleanliness triggers health inspector AI.

Host / Manager

Manages seating flow and customer experience. Reduces wait times. Higher-tier hosts boost overall satisfaction rating by greeting and managing queues.

Delivery Driver

Unlocks delivery revenue stream — customers who don't come in person. Requires investment in packaging. More drivers = more delivery capacity but diminishing returns.

Technology — Tiered Upgrades

Oven

Tiers: basic → commercial → industrial. Affects bake speed and batch size. Cheaper ovens have higher breakdown risk (AI curveball).

Register

Tiers: manual → POS → self-checkout. Multiplies cashier checkout speed. Self-checkout = no cashier needed but expensive upfront.

Refrigerator

Tiers by size: mini → standard → walk-in. Limits ingredient storage. Small fridge = can't stock enough for peak hours = lost sales.

Espresso Machine

Tiers: manual → semi-auto → full-auto. Affects drink quality and barista speed. Full-auto = faster but less artisan (lower satisfaction for specialty drinks).

Store Design — Adjustable

Redesignable Each Night

Unlike the original concept, store design can now be changed during closing hours — at a cost. Options include: modern minimalist, cozy rustic, industrial loft, classic diner. Each design has a different ambiance score affecting the model's satisfaction output. Redesigning costs budget but lets players adapt if their current look isn't drawing the right customers.

Menu System

Drinks: Tea, coffee, matcha latte, fresh juice, chai, smoothie. Each has cost, price, and satisfaction score.

Sweet baked: Croissant, cookie, cinnamon roll, baguette, muffin, brownie. Different production time and margins.

Savory baked: Egg bite, bagel, breakfast sandwich, savory scone, quiche, wrap. Higher price but need more chef time.

Menu can be expanded each night. More variety = more customer types attracted but higher ingredient costs and staff requirements.

Budget Features

Store hours: Each hour has a flat-rate operating tax. Diminishing returns late night.

Seating: More tables = less wait time = more revenue. But tables need bussers for turnover.

Advertisements: Channel spend + auction bids. More ads = more foot traffic but costs stack up.

Anti-AFK: Rent + staff wages + hourly tax paid every day whether you optimize or not. AFK players bleed cash fast.

Scoring

Primary: Cumulative net revenue across all rounds.

Secondary: Customer count, satisfaction avg, cleanliness score.

Learning Outcomes

Predictive modeling: Which features drive revenue? Model coefficients become intuitive through play.

Resource optimization: Budget tradeoffs force concentration on high-coefficient features.

Competitive strategy: Zone-based competition teaches market positioning. If everyone in Downtown buys ads, marginal return per dollar drops.

Risk management: Diversified menus, adequate staffing, and premium tech hedge against curveballs.

Dataset-Driven

Professor provides a festival dataset with features: artist tier, genre, ticket price, marketing, venue type, vendor count, staffing levels → resulting attendance/revenue. Players study this to understand which features have the highest model coefficients.

I

Interaction Terms

The model includes interaction terms — venue type × genre produces a multiplier effect. An outdoor field boosts EDM attendance more than country. Amphitheater × indie rock has a different coefficient. Players who discover these interactions from the data have a massive edge.

Key interaction term: venue × genre

Outdoor Field × EDM = high multiplier. Warehouse × Indie = high multiplier. Amphitheater × Pop = moderate. The "best venue" depends entirely on your genre pick. Studying the dataset reveals these combos.

attendance = β₁(venue) + β₂(genre) + β₃(venue × genre) + ...
Festival Frenzy

Lineup — Artist Tiers & Auction

Players pick individual artists from a roster, labeled by tier. A-tier headliners are auctioned off — highest bidder gets them. B and C tier are first-come or unlimited.

A-Tier Headliners

Limited supply — 5-8 A-tier artists available per weekend. Players bid in sealed auction to secure them. Highest bid wins. Massive draw but expensive. If your A-tier cancels (AI curveball), you're devastated.

Auctioned

B-Tier Artists

Moderate supply — 15-20 available. First-come allocation (faster planners get first pick). Good draw at lower cost. Less risk if one cancels. Solid ROI for budget-conscious players.

First-come

C-Tier / Local Acts

Unlimited supply. Low cost, low draw, but fills out lineup depth. More acts = higher genre diversity score in the model. Quantity play for players who spent their budget elsewhere.

Unlimited
Artist satisfaction — interaction term with cancellation risk

Each artist has a satisfaction variable that tracks how well they were treated (pay, amenities backstage, crowd size). Higher artist satisfaction = more likely to return in future weekends AND lower cancellation risk. Low satisfaction = higher chance the AI cancellation curveball targets them.

cancel_prob = base_rate × (1 - artist_satisfaction) → high sat = low cancel risk

Festival Frenzy

Features — Vendors, Staffing & Security

Expanded vendor types, staff roles with numerical counts, and security mechanics that directly affect revenue through sneaking prevention.

Food & Drink Vendors — Expanded

Hot Dog Stand

Low cost, high volume. Appeals to budget attendees. Set the number of stands to scale revenue.

Craft Beer Tent

High margin, moderate volume. Boosts satisfaction. Pairs well with certain genres (indie, country).

Gourmet Food Truck

High cost, high satisfaction. Premium per-attendee revenue. Limited capacity per truck — number matters.

Water / Juice Bar

Low margin but boosts safety/satisfaction. Required for outdoor venues in hot weather curveball.

Vendor staff: The more vendor staff you hire, the more you can yield from each vendor. Understaffed vendors = long lines = lost sales. Each vendor type has a staff-to-yield ratio.

Staffing — Number of Each Role

Security

Prevents people from sneaking in — every person who sneaks in is missed ticket revenue. More security = fewer gate crashers = more revenue captured. Required minimum, but optimal is higher.

Janitors

Directly increases attendee satisfaction score. Clean grounds = happier fans = higher satisfaction = loyalty bonus for repeat weekends. Underspending here tanks your satisfaction.

Medics

Required minimum. More medics = fewer incident penalties. Incidents reduce satisfaction and can trigger bad press curveball.

Stage Crew

Affects show quality. Understaffed stage crew = sound issues = lower artist satisfaction = higher cancellation risk next weekend.

Other Adjustables

Ticket Pricing

Set GA and VIP prices. Elasticity from dataset — too high = low attendance, too low = leaving money on the table. Sweet spot depends on lineup and competition.

Amenities

VIP lounges, art installations, phone charging, merch booths. Each has cost + satisfaction multiplier. High satisfaction = repeat attendance bonus in later rounds.

Advertisements

More ad spend = more viral moment probability. Ads directly feed the viral moment curveball — higher ad investment = higher chance of getting the positive viral boost.

Scoring

Primary: Cumulative net revenue across all rounds.

Audience splitting: Same region + genre = split demand. Attractiveness score determines your share. 6 EDM/West players = each gets ~1/6 base.

Anti-AFK: Venue lease + min staff costs paid every weekend. Idle players bleed. Can't profit without active decisions.

Learning Outcomes

Interaction terms: Venue × genre teaches that features don't act in isolation — combinations matter.

Linked curveballs: Artist cancellation ↔ satisfaction, viral ↔ ads, bad press ↔ cleanliness. Students learn that variables connect to each other, not just to the outcome.

Competitive positioning: Audience splitting teaches market strategy. Reading the room at setup is as important as optimizing numbers.
